🌍 The Essential Questions of Geography
지리학이 묻는 핵심 질문: '어디에'를 넘어서

One word is inextricably associated with geography: where.

That is because geography starts from the premise that it matters where something takes place on Earth's surface.

The key questions are not simply "where" questions, though; they are "why there" and "so what" questions.

Getting to such questions means taking spatial arrangements, variations, and interconnections seriously.

Engaging in even the simplest day-to-day activity requires some appreciation of spatial circumstances — where to find food and services, how to get to work places, and the like.

Moving up in scale, without some awareness of   how things are arranged  , it is difficult to make reasoned business or policy judgments, make sense of events, or grasp some of the basic forces shaping life on the planet.

Locating a new store or public service requires taking into consideration population distributions, the location of roads and utilities, socio-economic patterns, and more.

Understanding why and where migration happens requires consideration of the political organization of territory, the spatial consequences of discrimination, socio-economic patterns, and the layout of the physical environment.

📍 The Importance of Spatial Referents
공간적 데이터 수집 방법론: 정확한 공간 기준의 중요성

Perhaps nothing has revolutionized the study of human-environment interactions more than the development of spatially explicit methods for collecting and analyzing data.

Just a few decades ago, most natural and social scientists collected their data without precise spatial referents.

References were made to watersheds, to communities, to villages, to cities, to proximity to rivers, and so on.

In some cases (in anthropology mostly), there was even the custom of changing the name of the community and even its location, "to protect the informants."

While confidentiality remains an important concern when working with human subjects, the extreme to which this was done was largely unjustified.

Everyone knew more or less where these studied communities were, and anyone interested could have found out.

Be that as it may, there was very little interest, except in geography, to precisely locate a study area on a map with coordinates.

Geography persisted, and finally convinced the rest of us, that place was fundamentally important to understand human-environment dynamics.

🤔 Descartes' Philosophical Inquiry
데카르트와 감각의 기만: 의심 끝에 도달한 존재의 확신

The idea that our senses may be deceiving us was taken up by the French philosopher and mathematician René Descartes.

(A) It might even be that we are asleep, and that the world we are experiencing is no more than a dream, or that God or some mischievous power is making us believe things that are not really true.

If that is the case, though, we can never know anything for sure.

(B) But Descartes then showed that there was one thing he could be sure of: In order to be deceived, he must exist, and exist as a thing that thinks.

So, although his senses could not be trusted, he could be sure he existed as something that was capable of thinking and reasoning.

(C) He realized that sometimes our senses give us a false impression — a straight stick in a glass of water appears to be bent, for example.

And if we know that they can be unreliable, it would be wrong to trust them completely — everything that we experience with our senses might be false.

Chapter 21 · 3번 실전 문제
🍖 Resource Sharing as a Survival Strategy
아체족의 자원 공유: 생존을 위한 상호 보험

Hunters in the Ache tribe, living in the Paraguayan jungle, have a lot of ups and downs in their success at the hunt.

Some days they bring home much more food than they could possibly eat; other days they come home empty-handed.

Instead of living by a philosophy of "rugged individualism," however, hunters who have a lucky day share their meat with other families.

( ① ) If a man caught a wild pig and hoarded it for himself and his family, much of it would go to waste (there are no deep-freeze refrigerators in the Paraguayan jungle).

( ② ) During unlucky periods, individual hunters and their families would starve.

( ③ ) And they don't just share a little; they share a lot — fully 90%.

( ④ ) In exchange for this generosity, their neighbors share with them on days when the luck runs the other way.

( ⑤ ) By exchanging resources, particularly those that are unpredictable and uncertain (like meat from the hunt, as opposed to vegetable crops), the group provides a mutual insurance policy against starvation.

03 Topic & 04 Logical Framework
* Topic: resource sharing as a collective survival strategy among the Ache tribe
* Text Structure
Claim Ache hunters experience highly unpredictable outcomes — feast or famine.
Support 1 Hoarding individually would lead to waste on good days, starvation on bad days.
Support 2 Instead, hunters share generously, up to 90% of their catch / meat.
Support 3 Neighbors reciprocate the benefit on unlucky days.
Conclusion Resource sharing functions as a collective insurance policy against starvation.
* main idea
Because hunting yields are variable, Ache hunters share resources extensively with neighboring families rather than hoarding individually, creating a cooperative system that protects the whole group against starvation.
